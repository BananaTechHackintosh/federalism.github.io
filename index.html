Basic Structure & Definition

What kind of government is federalism?

Federalism is a system of government where power is divided and shared between a central (national) government and regional (state or provincial) governments. Both levels have real power. Neither is just a “branch office” of the other.

Type of government

System
In a federal system like the United States, the overall government is usually a constitutional democracy. People choose leaders at multiple levels:
  • National government (for example, President, Congress)
  • State governments (governors, state legislatures)
  • Local governments (mayors, councils, school boards)
The written constitution sets the rules and limits all of them.

Who makes the laws?

Law-making
Laws come from both the national legislature and the state legislatures, and are ultimately based on the will of the people:
  • Citizens vote to choose lawmakers and executives.
  • National and state governments pass laws within their powers.
  • Courts review laws to make sure they follow the Constitution.
The result: shared power instead of a single ruler.

Historical Development

How federalism took shape in the United States

American federalism grew out of conflict and experimentation. The goal: create a government strong enough to protect freedom but limited enough not to destroy it.

Key phases of American federalism

Timeline
Colonial era Early hints of shared power British colonies had their own assemblies and governors, giving Americans early experience with divided government authority.
1776–1781 Revolution & Articles of Confederation After the American Revolution, the new states created the Articles of Confederation, a loose union with a very weak national government—too weak to solve major problems.
1787–1789 Constitutional Convention James Madison and other leaders designed a new Constitution that created a stronger federal government while still protecting state powers—this is the full birth of U.S. federalism.
Civil War era Union & federal supremacy The Civil War proved that the United States is one nation, not just a group of states. Afterwards, the national government gained more power to protect rights, especially against slavery.
20th century Dual, cooperative, and new federalism U.S. federalism evolved from dual federalism (clear separation of state and national powers) to cooperative federalism under Franklin D. Roosevelt and the New Deal, where both levels worked together on big issues like the Great Depression. In the 1970s, presidents like Richard Nixon encouraged “new federalism”, shifting some power and flexibility back to the states.

Influential leaders & ideas

Thinkers

Several leaders shaped how federalism works in the United States:

  • James Madison – One of the main authors of the Constitution and the Federalist Papers. He argued for dividing power so no single level or branch could dominate.
  • Franklin D. Roosevelt – Led the New Deal, which used federal programs and funding to fight the Great Depression, expanding cooperative federalism.
  • Richard Nixon – Promoted “new federalism,” giving states more control and flexibility over how to use federal funds.

Distribution & Use of Power

Who holds power in a federal system?

Instead of power piling up in one place, federalism spreads it across levels of government and branches of government, all ultimately accountable to the people.

Levels of power

Shared authority
Power is divided among:
  • National (federal) government – Handles issues that affect the whole country: defense, currency, immigration, foreign policy, and national economic policy.
  • State governments – Manage education, local transportation, public safety, elections, and many social policies.
  • Local governments – Cities, counties, and towns handle community needs: police, fire protection, zoning, local roads, and parks.

Each level has its own responsibilities, but they also cooperate and sometimes even argue—this tension is part of how federalism protects freedom.

Checks, balances, and elections

Prevention of abuse
Federalism works together with the three-branch system:
  • Legislative – Makes laws (Congress, state legislatures).
  • Executive – Enforces laws (President, governors, mayors).
  • Judicial – Interprets laws and can strike down unconstitutional ones (courts).

Leaders are chosen by elections or, in some systems, by appointment. Courts, legislatures, and the people themselves all check each other’s power, making it harder for anyone to become a dictator.

Citizens & Participation

How do people take part in a federal system?

In a federal democracy, government is not something done to the people—it is something done by the people, at many levels.

Rights and responsibilities

Freedom
Citizens in a federal democracy typically enjoy:
  • Voting rights – Choosing leaders at national, state, and local levels.
  • Freedom of speech – Criticizing leaders and policies without fear of punishment.
  • Freedom of religion – Government cannot force a single religion or ban peaceful beliefs.
  • Equality under the law – Protection against discrimination based on race, religion, or other unfair reasons.

With these rights comes responsibility: staying informed, obeying just laws, serving on juries, and participating in community life.

Ways to be heard

Participation
People influence government in multiple ways:
  • Voting in elections at every level.
  • Contacting representatives and attending public meetings.
  • Joining parties, interest groups, or community organizations.
  • Peaceful protest, petition, and social media advocacy.

Because decisions are made at many levels, it is often easier for citizens to get involved close to home and see results quickly.

Economy & Social Services

How does a federal system handle money and services?

Federalism lets different levels of government share the cost and work of running a modern society—schools, roads, health care, and more.

Taxes and budgets

Public money
In many federal systems, including the United States:
  • The federal government collects income and excise taxes to fund national defense, social security, major health programs, and other nationwide priorities.
  • States and local governments collect sales, income, and property taxes to fund schools, police and fire departments, local roads, and community services.
  • The federal government often provides grants to states to support education, health care, and infrastructure, sometimes with rules attached.

Services that affect everyday life

Public services
Federalism helps match services to the level where they work best:
Mostly national
  • National defense
  • Major highways and transportation networks
  • National health programs (like Medicare/Medicaid in the U.S.)
  • Nationwide economic and environmental rules
Mostly state/local
  • K–12 schools and local colleges
  • Local roads, public transit, and zoning
  • Police, fire, and emergency services
  • Local welfare and housing programs

By dividing responsibilities, federalism allows policies to be tailored to local needs while still meeting national standards.

Cultural & Religious Foundations

What values shape a federal system?

Federalism does not require one religion or culture. Instead, it depends on shared political values and a commitment to live together under common rules.

Core ideas

Principles
Some of the key values behind modern federalism include:
  • Freedom – Especially freedoms of speech, religion, press, and assembly.
  • Equality – All people deserve equal protection and fair treatment under the law.
  • Pluralism – Different states and communities can have different policies while still being part of one country.
  • Rule of law – Leaders must obey the same laws as everyone else, and those laws come from a higher constitution.

Religion and tradition may shape people’s beliefs, but in a federal democracy the government itself is not supposed to favor one faith. Instead, it protects the freedom of all.

Modern Implementation & Challenges

Is federalism working today?

Federalism remains a powerful way to balance unity and diversity, but it is not perfect. Its strengths and weaknesses show up in real debates over rights, safety, and freedom.

Strengths of federalism

Benefits
  • Protects liberty by preventing any one level of government from gaining total control.
  • Encourages participation by giving citizens many different elections and governments to influence.
  • Allows experimentation: states can try different solutions and learn from each other’s successes and mistakes.
  • Fits a large, diverse country where people and regions may want different policies while still staying united.

Ongoing challenges

Tensions
Federalism also raises hard questions:
  • How much freedom should states have when their laws affect national rights or equality?
  • What happens when state and federal laws conflict?
  • How do we prevent “too much” freedom from turning into chaos or unfair treatment?
  • How can we keep government responsive to people while still solving big national problems?

These debates are not a sign that federalism is failing—they are a sign that the system is actively balancing power and freedom.
